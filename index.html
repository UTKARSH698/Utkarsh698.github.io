§ 03 From the editor

A note on the work.

I build infrastructure for the moments when it has to keep working — when traffic spikes, when a region goes down, when a payment gateway returns errors at 3am. The interesting engineering isn't in the happy path; it's in what happens after the first failure.

This issue collects eight pieces — six systems I’ve shipped, one merged upstream contribution, and one formal-methods preprint. A distributed SAGA orchestrator on AWS Step Functions, a real-time Lambda-architecture analytics platform on AWS Kinesis, a security posture agent that audits AWS accounts against CIS v1.5, a six-module Gemini AI platform with payments and OTP behind a single Express server, an event-photography matcher that returns only the photos a guest appears in by indexing faces with InsightFace, a full retrieval-augmented QA pipeline with source-diversity guarantees and an LLM-as-judge evaluator, a small but real PR into grafana/alloy-scenarios that closed a long-standing local-emulator gap — and a TLA⁺-style proof that three RBAC security invariants, each correct per service, break under cross-service concurrency.

Each one is built to a standard I'd be willing to put on call. The architecture, the trade-offs, and the parts I'd do differently are all written out. If any of it is useful or you want to talk about something similar, the contact page is at the back.

— Utkarsh Batham, Vadodara · 2026

§ 04 Statement of intent M.Sc. Applications · Fall 2027

I want to do an M.Sc. in computer science focused on the security and verification of distributed cloud systems — the same territory my independent research opens up. The interesting questions for me sit at the seam between formal methods and production cloud-native architecture: how do we state, enforce, and prove security invariants on systems whose components fail independently? I’m looking for groups working on cloud security, policy verification, and distributed-systems reliability — and a programme where the research connects back to the platforms people actually run.
